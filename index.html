Shooting Your Initial Azimuth

    Get Started.

    Go here to view my Mentor's blog. He has many tips to help you become that interesting interviewee with a love for tech that the big companies are looking for. His help has been invaluable to me on my personal journey. Check him out, you won't regret it.

    From the Soapbox

      I was told by the "experts" that I needed to apply for other "non-coding" positions in tech; they said, "There's no way you can compete with the CS grads coming out of college". I was told that I was a potential 'fit', but not for development. I heard all of that garbage and noise, and I thought, "So what! I'm still going to be a developer." So, Here I am, 8 months later, about to start my second programming gig with a great company after being successful on the job at my first gig. Don't take no for an answer. Work hard. Believe in yourself. You can do it. Getting off of my soapbox, now.

    Code. Read. Network. Eat. Poop. Sleep. (in that order)

      Consume Podcasts and Audiobooks on your daily commute.

      The two buddies that I learned this practice from (albeit, late in the game), currently work for Microsoft, making big boy change.

      Read as much as you can when away from the screen.

      Here is a short list, by no means comprehensive. See what your buddies are reading. Take recommendations. Just keep expanding your mind and constantly pulling in new information. I recently read "Mindset" by Carol Dweck. Take her advice and embrace a "growth mindset".

      Make friends with programmers.

      Learn the insider lingo. Do as many informational interviews as you can handle off of LinkedIn. Just inbox them and display an earnest desire to learn. Works a lot more than you think.

      Challenge Yourself Daily.

      Document the memorable challenges you face, and be prepared to talk about them in STAR format. Keep a journal of your challenges, and have a whole bunch of them ready to whip out on your next phone screen or interview

      White Board.

      Get one. Hang it on your wall. Start solving some algorithm-related problems armed with only your logic as a tool. Pseudo-code your way to a solution. Research the code you will need. Solve the problem. Rinse. Lather. Repeat. As you grow, you will drop the research step, and with every problem solved, you become a better programmer. Talk while you code on the white board to your imaginary audience. Explain WHY you are doing this step or that one. Do it with a friend trying to sharpshoot you. This is, by far, the most helpful practice to get better. I still do it every day. Even at work, with production problems.

Coding Resources that I've Used

    Pluralsight

    Subscription-based service ($29/month) that is at or near the pinnacle of coding tutorials. Very high quality content. You can get a 90-day free subscription when signed into your Microsoft account here. Look up Mosh Hamedani. I am a big fan of his. Thank me later.

    Udemy

    Offers cheap and high-quality coding tutorials and courses on the latest technologies. Once you pay for a course, it is yours forever. There are numerous and frequent discount promotions, so never pay over fifteen dollars for any course.

    Mozilla Developer Network

    Comprehensive Holy Trinity of Front-End Design (JavaScript, CSS, and HTML) online encyclopedia. When in doubt, look it up! (then check Stack Overflow) here.

    MSDN

    Comprehensive C# and .NET online encyclopedia. When in doubt, look it up! (then check Stack Overflow) here.

    freeCodeCamp

    Amazing community of developers and aspiring developers with four (FREE!) certs that can be earned with a lot of hard work, study and application. Going through the challenges are a lot of fun when you solve them. Very rewarding and addicting. You can do it! Hit me up if you get stuck! At the present, I am about 60 percent done with the Front-End Certification requirements.

    Codecademy

    Interactive learning website that offers a lot of free content, along with *better* paid content for $19/month. Use it to grasp the fundamentals. Programming is programming, regardless of the language you learn.

    HackerRank

    A personal favorite of mine. This site is awesome, because you can do coding challenges in just about ANY language. Challenges are fun, can be hard at times, but are extremely rewarding once you figure out the logic and apply the code that fits that algorithm. Get after It!

    Microsoft Virtual Academy

    Get started with Bob Tabor's excellent C# Fundamentals for Absolute Beginners and then branch out to whereever you want. MVA offers a lot of great content, all free.

    Dot Net Perls

    Sample excerpt: For. A gust of wind appears. A leaf travels through the air. In nature these phenomena are all around us. Our world is full of motion. This website explains C# in detail, in a way that is easily understandable, with plenty of code snippets to sample. Very good resource.

Stumped? Get help!

    Stack Overflow

    Stuck?? Make this your first stop...after exhausting your own research. Be detailed, include the code that you are having issues with. Be humble, grasshopper. It's usually Google's top hit when you describe a bug in detail. Chances are, someone else observed and solved your problem already. If they haven't, you can post and ask!

    Google It!

    Not too many questions the internets can't answer.

    Quora

    Lots of great discussions about anything and everything. Lot of computer science stuff being discussed here.

For Veterans or Transitioning Service Members

    Microsoft Software & Systems Academy

    Amazing program that you can use your VA benefits to attend, while you are still in. You must get permission from your chain-of-command to attend the program before you ETS or retire.

    Amazon Military Recruiting

    Amazon has an initiative to train and hire veterans. Learn more about it at the preceding link.

    Onward to Opportunity - Syracuse U.

    Another veteran's vocational program. I am not aware of all the benefits, but if you are interested, here's a link to their information.

    Operation Code

    from the website: The largest community dedicated to helping military veterans and families launch software development careers. I, personally, didn't take full advantage of their services, but a lot of my very successful colleagues cannot say enough good things about this organization and what they have to offer us.

About Me

My Roots

I am a former Army Paratrooper with twenty years of service to our great country. I served most of my time at Fort Bragg, NC in the 82nd Airborne Division. I retired as a Sergeant First Class, and treasure all of the people that have helped me along the way during my career. When transitioning out of the military, I happened upon a transition program that changed my life. I participated in the first Microsoft Software and Systems Academy Cohort on Fort Bragg. I met many influential people, learned to believe in my ability to thrive in tech, and had a lot of fun in my journey to become the person I am today during the course. I owe much of my success to this program, and highly recommend it to any transitioning service member or veteran. I am a family man, with three sons aged 17, 7, and 2. I also have a daughter from another daddy, aged 13, that am just as proud of. I enjoy spending time with family, having a cocktail or two, and enjoying the outdoors, especially the beach.

My Present

I am a Junior Software Developer with three months of total experience working with production code. I owe my last employer, The Oryza Group, a huge debt of gratitude for having faith in a programmer with zero experience. I really enjoyed working there, and would recommend the environment to anyone looking to break into a developer role. I have recently accepted an offer with Booz Allen Hamilton as a Software Developer II in Atlanta, GA. I am currently in the process of moving, and am very excited about joining a new team in a new city!

My Future

I think the future is what you make it with your actions today. I plan to grind as hard as I can, absorb all of the information I can consume, and just fulfill the goal of becoming a better developer, one day at a time, one step at a time, with the goal of becoming someone who excels in his craft. I see myself staying in the Peach State for a long time, and exploring the possibilities that becoming a world-class Dev may bring. My dream is to collaborate on something noble and useful that ordinary people can use to improve their lives on a daily basis, someday. Good luck to you on your journey, and check back with me if you want an update on mine! Take care and Godspeed to you!
